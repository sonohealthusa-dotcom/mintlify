title: "FDA-Cleared vs. FDA-Approved: What It Means for Home Health Devices"

---

When shopping for home health devices you'll see phrases like "FDA-cleared" and "FDA-approved" — and they don't mean the same thing. Understanding the difference helps you judge what you're actually buying.

<Note>
Regulatory status tells you a device met a defined standard for its intended use. It doesn't make any device a substitute for professional medical care.
</Note>

## What does FDA-cleared mean?

**FDA clearance** (via the 510(k) pathway) applies to many low-to-moderate-risk medical devices. It means the manufacturer demonstrated the device is **substantially equivalent** to a legally marketed device — safe and effective for its intended use. Most home devices like fetal dopplers, blood pressure monitors, and EKG monitors fall here. SonoHealth's **HeartBeats** fetal doppler is FDA-cleared.

## What does FDA-approved mean?

**FDA approval** is a more rigorous pathway (premarket approval, or PMA) generally reserved for higher-risk devices, and the term is also used for drugs. It typically requires clinical evidence of safety and effectiveness. Some home devices reference approval depending on their classification — for example, SonoHealth describes the **MistPro** nebulizer as FDA-approved.

## Why does the distinction matter when buying?

Both clearance and approval mean a device has been through an FDA process for its intended use, which is a meaningful signal of quality — especially compared with unregulated gadgets. The key is that the device makes its claim honestly: be wary of vague "FDA registered" language, which only refers to a facility listing and is **not** the same as clearance or approval.

## How do I verify a device's status?

Reputable brands state their regulatory status plainly and consistently. You can also look up cleared and approved devices in the FDA's public databases. For SonoHealth specifically, see the [fetal doppler FDA clearance](/fetal-doppler/fda-clearance) and [nebulizer FDA approval](/nebulizer/fda-approval) pages.

## Does cleared or approved mean "doctor optional"?

No. Regulatory status describes the device, not a replacement for care. Home medical devices are designed to **support** monitoring and treatment plans — diagnosis and treatment decisions still belong to a clinician.
