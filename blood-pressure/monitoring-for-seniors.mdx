Blood pressure becomes more important to watch with age, and home monitoring is one of the most useful things older adults and their caregivers can do between appointments. The keys are an easy-to-use device, good technique, and a clear log to share with the doctor.

<Note>
Home readings support — but never replace — professional care. Don't change any medication based on home numbers; bring your log to your clinician instead.
</Note>

## Why does home monitoring matter more with age?

Blood pressure patterns change over the years, and high blood pressure becomes more common — yet it still usually has no symptoms. Regular home readings catch trends an occasional office visit would miss and help your doctor fine-tune care. They also help spot the kind of swings that can lead to falls.

## What is isolated systolic hypertension?

In older adults it's common for the **top number (systolic) to be high while the bottom number (diastolic) is normal** — called isolated systolic hypertension. It's driven by stiffening of the arteries with age and is still worth managing. Your doctor sets the target; your job is consistent measurement. See [understanding systolic and diastolic](/blood-pressure/understanding-systolic-diastolic).

## What monitor features help seniors most?

Prioritize a simple **automatic upper-arm monitor** with one-button operation, a large high-contrast display, and reading memory. The most important detail is a **correctly sized cuff** — measure the arm and confirm fit, since a poor fit skews results (see [cuff size and accuracy](/blood-pressure/cuff-size-and-accuracy)). SonoHealth's **BPpro** and **BPMAX** are upper-arm monitors built for straightforward use.

## What about dizziness when standing up?

Feeling lightheaded on standing can signal [orthostatic hypotension](/blood-pressure/low-blood-pressure), which is more common with age and raises fall risk. Rising slowly, staying hydrated, and noting these episodes in your log all help. Mention frequent dizziness to the doctor, since some medications contribute.

## How can a caregiver help?

Caregivers can take readings at consistent times, keep a tidy [log the doctor can use](/blood-pressure/morning-vs-evening-readings), and watch for irregular-heartbeat flags. Measuring the same way each day makes the numbers comparable and meaningful.
