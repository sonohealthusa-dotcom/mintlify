It's one of the most repeated pieces of pregnancy folklore: a fast heartbeat means a girl, a slow one means a boy. It's a fun guessing game — but it isn't supported by evidence. Here's what the fetal heart rate actually tells you.

<Note>
A home fetal doppler is a bonding and reassurance tool, not a diagnostic or gender-prediction device. It cannot confirm your baby's wellbeing or sex.
</Note>

## The myth

The idea that fetal heart rate predicts sex — often quoted as "above 140 is a girl, below 140 is a boy" — is a popular old wives' tale. Studies looking at fetal heart rate and sex have not found a reliable difference, especially in early pregnancy when the myth is most often invoked.

## What actually affects fetal heart rate

A baby's heart rate normally ranges roughly **110–160 beats per minute**, and it changes constantly based on:

- **Gestational age** — heart rate trends shift as pregnancy progresses
- **The baby's activity** — moving versus resting
- **The moment you happen to measure**

None of these is sex. For what a typical reading looks like, see [heartbeat normal ranges](/fetal-doppler/heartbeat-normal-ranges).

## How sex is actually determined

A baby's sex is determined by chromosomes and is identified through **ultrasound** (typically around the anatomy scan) or **genetic testing** (such as NIPT) — not by listening to the heartbeat. A handheld doppler simply can't provide that information.

## What a home doppler is really for

The [HeartBeats Fetal Doppler](/fetal-doppler/overview) is an [FDA-cleared](/fetal-doppler/fda-clearance) device for **bonding and reassurance** — a way to share a moment with a partner between appointments. Used [responsibly](/fetal-doppler/safety), that can be a lovely ritual. Just remember it's not a window into your baby's sex or a substitute for prenatal care.
