## Is Home Fetal Doppler Ultrasound Safe?

The HeartBeats™ Fetal Doppler is **FDA cleared**, operating at 2.5 MHz at power levels substantially lower than clinical diagnostic ultrasound machines. Major medical organizations and the FDA classify consumer fetal dopplers as safe when used as directed — briefly and intermittently rather than continuously for extended periods.

## FDA Position on Home Fetal Dopplers

The FDA classifies fetal dopplers as **Class II medical devices** (the same class as powered wheelchairs and hearing aids). The HeartBeats™ device has received FDA clearance, meaning it has passed the agency's substantial equivalence review against predicate devices with established safety profiles.

The FDA advises that home fetal dopplers should be used prudently:
- Not as a substitute for professional prenatal care
- Not to "rule out" fetal distress (they are not diagnostic tools)
- Not continuously for extended sessions

## Ultrasound at 2.5 MHz: What the Research Says

Diagnostic ultrasound has been used in obstetrics since the 1960s. The safety profile of obstetric ultrasound is one of the most studied topics in perinatal medicine.

**Key safety parameters:**
- **Thermal Index (TI):** Measures potential tissue heating. Home dopplers operate at TI < 1.0, where no thermal effect on fetal tissue has been demonstrated
- **Mechanical Index (MI):** Measures cavitation risk. Home dopplers operate well below the 0.3 threshold for cavitation effects
- **ALARA Principle:** "As Low As Reasonably Achievable" — medical ultrasound guidelines recommend minimizing exposure time and output. Home dopplers comply by design.

Dozens of epidemiological studies have followed children whose mothers used diagnostic ultrasound during pregnancy. No studies have found adverse developmental outcomes attributable to ultrasound at clinical or consumer device power levels.

## How to Use HeartBeats™ Safely

**Recommended practices:**
- Keep individual sessions brief — 5–10 minutes at a time
- Use only after 12 weeks of pregnancy (fetal heart detection typically begins at 10–14 weeks depending on position, maternal anatomy, and gestational weight)
- Apply ultrasound gel to improve signal quality and reduce probe pressure
- Do not press the probe forcefully against the abdomen — light, consistent contact is sufficient
- Do not attempt to use as a substitute for professional monitoring

**Do not use as a diagnostic tool:**
A home doppler can confirm that a heartbeat is present, but it cannot:
- Assess heart rate variability (HRV) adequately
- Detect non-reassuring heart rate patterns
- Diagnose intrauterine growth restriction, cord accidents, or placental insufficiency

## When to Call Your Doctor Instead

Do not use a home doppler to self-reassure when you notice the following symptoms — contact your healthcare provider immediately:

- **Reduced or absent fetal movement** (after 24–28 weeks when movement is established)
- **Vaginal bleeding**
- **Severe abdominal pain or cramping**
- **Leaking fluid**
- **Signs of preeclampsia** (severe headache, vision changes, facial swelling)

A home doppler finding a heartbeat in these scenarios does NOT mean everything is fine. These symptoms require clinical evaluation regardless of doppler findings.
