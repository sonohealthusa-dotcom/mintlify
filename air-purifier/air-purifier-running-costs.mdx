Air purifiers work best running continuously, so a common question before buying is what that does to your electric bill. For most modern HEPA purifiers, the answer is reassuringly little — the bigger ongoing cost is filters.

<Note>
Energy use varies by model, fan speed, and local electricity rates, so treat these as general guidelines rather than exact figures for any one home.
</Note>

## Is it expensive to run an air purifier 24/7?

Generally no. Consumer air purifiers draw modest power, especially on lower and night settings, so running one continuously typically adds only a small amount to a monthly bill. Higher fan speeds use more energy than [Night Mode](/air-purifier/smart-features), so a unit that runs quietly and efficiently at low speed keeps costs down.

## What about filter replacement costs?

Filters are usually the larger ongoing expense. The AirPro's HEPA 14 filter lasts roughly [6–12 months](/air-purifier/filter-replacement) depending on air quality and runtime, and [replacement filters](/air-purifier/filter-replacement) are priced from $29.99 for a single pack. A [filter subscription](/air-purifier/filter-replacement) takes 20% off and ships every 90 days, and it includes a lifetime warranty.

## What makes filters wear out faster?

Heavy use during events like [wildfire smoke](/air-purifier/wildfire-smoke-guide), homes with [multiple pets](/air-purifier/for-pets), heavy dust, or running on the highest setting all shorten filter life. The AirPro's smart tracking estimates remaining filter life so you replace it on time rather than guessing.

## How do I keep total running costs low?

- Run on a lower or Night Mode setting when air quality is good.
- Clean the **pre-filter** regularly so the main filter lasts longer.
- Use a [subscription](/air-purifier/filter-replacement) to save on filters.
- Size the unit to the room so it doesn't run flat-out — see [room coverage](/air-purifier/room-coverage).
