# Why Source Matters in a Magnesium Supplement

When most people shop for magnesium, they focus on two things: the form (glycinate, citrate, oxide) and the dose. Both matter. But there's a third factor that often gets overlooked: **where the magnesium comes from**.

Source determines what comes *with* the magnesium — and that matters more than most people realize.

## The Problem With Sourcing Opacity

Pick up most magnesium supplements and the ingredient label reads something like:

> "Magnesium (as magnesium glycinate) — 400mg"

That tells you the form and the dose. It tells you nothing about:
- Where the raw magnesium originated
- What was processed out vs. preserved
- Whether the source environment was clean and tested
- Whether anything naturally co-occurs with the magnesium in the final product

This opacity isn't a sign of a bad product — it's just the way commodity supplements are typically formulated and labeled. The magnesium works regardless of whether you know which mine or brine well it came from.

But it does mean that most magnesium supplements are functionally interchangeable at the source level. A magnesium glycinate from one company's facility is chemically identical to one from another's, because both start with the same commodity magnesium oxide and react it with glycine. The ingredient is real; the source story is invisible.

## Does Source Change How Well Magnesium Works?

The honest answer: the primary driver of magnesium efficacy is form and dose, not source. Magnesium glycinate from a mined source works very well. Magnesium from a marine source works very well. The magnesium biochemistry is the same.

Where source becomes meaningfully relevant:

- **Trace mineral cofactors** — if you care about the mineral context around your magnesium
- **Sourcing transparency** — if provenance and traceability matter to you
- **Overall product philosophy** — if you prefer ingredients that are closer to nature vs. lab-processed

Think of it like protein sources. Whey protein from grass-fed cows and conventional whey both deliver the same amino acids. But the source story, the fatty acid profile, and the consumer's values may make one the preferred choice even when the core nutrient is equivalent.
